The official's journey — from sign-in link to decision

An MDA officer (e.g. on the Ministry of Youth team running the science summer camp) processes applications in the case management system: sign in by emailed link, work a team-scoped queue, read each case, ask for anything missing, and set the status. Mapped from the CMS sandbox walkthrough — including what the system doesn't yet do for them: no notifications, no assignment, and a status model that may not match how their program really decides.

Pain pointsWhere the journey breaks

Inbox-dependent access

Every sign-in routes through email; no SSO. Fine for occasional use, friction for daily casework.

No notifications, no assignment

Nobody is told when a case arrives or a response comes back; “who gets notified, who's assigned” is still undesigned. SLA display is decorative.

Unreadable form data

The case page dumps the form payload in one jumbled block — slow to assess, easy to miss things.

Can't collect documents mid-case

If the missing thing is a certificate rather than an answer, this loop can't carry it.

Statuses don't match real workflows

Batch allocation (wait → allocate → approve all) and multi-step MDA processes don't fit received/accepted/rejected — and their decision sends the applicant no message.

Communication happens off-system

Telling applicants the outcome, if it happens at all, happens outside the CMS — untracked and unrepeatable.

What serves staff well today

  • Automatic intake — submissions file themselves under the right program via the form code; no transcription, no sorting.
  • Team-scoped queues — access controls keep each team's caseload separate within an MDA.
  • The info-request loop — ask, and the answer comes back onto the case with a full timeline.
  • “View applicant page” — empathy built in: see what the citizen sees before you act.
  • CSV export for anything the system doesn't do yet.

The core problem

  • The CMS is a good inbox but not yet a workflow tool: no notifications, no assignment, no real SLAs, and a four-state model that programs are already outgrowing.
  • Staff decisions are silent — approving or rejecting changes a page the applicant was never told about, so the accountability for communicating falls back on staff, off-system.

Questions to answer next

  • How do program leads actually decide (rolling vs batch) — the conversation planned with Justin?
  • Who gets notified on the staff side, and what does assignment look like?
  • Which intermediate statuses are shared across MDAs vs genuinely local — and where's the line before it becomes a workflow engine?
