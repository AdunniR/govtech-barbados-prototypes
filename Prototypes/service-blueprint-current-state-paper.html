Temporary restaurant licence & Environmental Health Officer request — as it works today

Ministry of Health — Environmental Health Department. The paper journey using forms A14:086 and A14:410, where the entire process — application, review, inspection and collection — happens in person at the polyclinic responsible for the event location's catchment. Randall Phillips runs an unofficial Google Forms workaround; every other polyclinic is fully paper-based.

No single front door

No central content page. Requirements, the two-week rule and the catchment system are learned by phoning a polyclinic, visiting, or asking around.

Pain point

Paper forms A14:086 & A14:410

Dotted-line forms filled at the front desk: applicant details, event, period, foods and source, contact numbers, handwritten signature. The officer request goes “through the Principal EHO”.

Randall Phillips only: Google Form

An online form with auto-generated licence — capped at ~31 free automated emails/month, then manual download-and-send.

Workaround

No receipt or reference

Nothing standard to show an application is in progress; status checks happen by phoning the polyclinic.

Pain point

Officer at the premises

The inspection visit itself is the only visible signal that the application is moving.

Paper licence

Signed and stamped at the polyclinic, collected in person. Late applications: hand-delivered on site on event day.

Pain point

Licence at the stall

Paper licence displayed; no way to verify authenticity beyond the stamp.

Files & officer reports

Paper files at each polyclinic; Randall Phillips keeps a spreadsheet backed up daily to a share drive and thumb drive.

Officers at the event

Inspect every food stall, sanitation and waste disposal; discretion over late or unlicensed vendors — lenient where safe, closure where not.

No follow-up loop

No systematic communication back to applicants; awareness relies on the same informal channels next season.

BackstagePolyclinic & EHD staff

Redirecting misdirected applicants

Pain point

Clerk logs the paper form

Office clerk records the application; the file physically moves clerk → senior EHO. At RPPC, Mr Taylor and the office manager alone hold access to the Google Form back end.

Workaround

Senior review

Senior EHO checks: existing licensed food business → fast track; otherwise inspection needed. Food prepared outside the catchment → letter/call to the neighbouring polyclinic for assistance.

Officer allocation

Senior assigns officers for inspections and site visits around existing workload; findings recorded in written officer reports.

Two-signature approval on paper

Senior recommends → Principal EHO signs (a “rubber stamp” by agreement with the MOH for temporary permits). Clerk phones the applicant to collect.

Event-day discretion

Officers deliver late licences on site and judge borderline stalls case by case — knowing vendors have already spent on raw materials.

Pain point

Manual reporting

Monthly and quarterly figures compiled by hand from paper files for the Chief, Minister and PS. Outbreak traceback means pulling paper reports.

Typed/stamped licences; RPPC's extension auto-generates a licence with a barcode but hits the 31-per-month email cap.

Data trapped on paper

Only RPPC can analyse foods sold or produce comparative officer statistics; the Ministry wants off Google entirely.

Pain point

The cost of the current process

  • Two trips minimum for every vendor — apply in person, return to collect — sometimes across the island, because the event's catchment decides the polyclinic.
  • Peak-season crunch: Crop Over and Christmas spikes hit a fully manual intake at some polyclinics.
  • No status visibility: every "where's my licence?" is a phone call for staff to answer.
  • Late applications cascade: vendors booked days out → licences hand-delivered on event day → officers forced into on-the-spot refusals.
  • Fee friction: organizers exposed to individual officer rates negotiate for cheaper juniors.

The Randall Phillips workaround

  • A Google Form with an extension that generates a barcoded licence and emails it — over 1,000 applications processed since it was set up.
  • Capped at ~31 free automated emails per month; extras are downloaded and sent manually.
  • Access belongs to individuals (Mr Taylor + office manager), not the post — it doesn't survive staff rotation and exists at one polyclinic only.
  • Data exported daily to a share drive and thumb drive for backup.
  • Proof of demand and of the concept — “a band-aid until we get something” — and the reason EHD approached GovTech.
